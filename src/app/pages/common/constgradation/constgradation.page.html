Consonant gradation & Vowel Harmony
In Finnish, the vowel are distinguished into 3 types :
- Back vowels :   A, O, U
- Neutral vowels :  E, I
- Front vowels :   Ä, Ö, Y

A verb, and more generally a word, (unless it is a compound word) can be only be composed according to the following rules :
- Back vowel words can only be composed of back and neutral vowels, e.g. he haluavat (could not be haluavät)
- Front vowel words can only be composed of front and neutral vowels, e.g. he häiritsevät (could not be häiritsevat)
- Otherwise if the word is only composed of neutral vowel, it will be assigned with front vowels, e.g. he sihisevät

Another rule to consider when conjugating is that there can't be more than 2 of the same vowel in a row.
Ex.: herätä (vt 4, to wake up) -> Hän heräää is not possible so it will be hän herää.
Vowel harmony in verbs

There are only a few irregular verb in Finnish however when you conjugate verbs, many of them are affected by the so-called "consonant gradation" in some forms. The change between weak and strong grade occurs in between the 2nd and 3rd syllable from the end of the verb. The strong grade becomes weak in indicative present and imperfect in all the persons except the 3rd persons (sg. & pl.) in case A and in all the persons in case B. In the case B, the weak grade becomes strong but the consonant gradation is not systematic. For example, tallata conjugates tallaan, tallaat, etc... .
